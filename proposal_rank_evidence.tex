\title{\textbf{GPT Rank as Evidence Against Higher-Order Interference}\\[0.5em]
\author{Tristan Lismer, Ro Lee, Kevin Resch}}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We propose a matrix-rank-based test for higher-order (Sorkin) interference using
multi-slit optical experiments.  The Born rule requires that all multi-slit
interference decomposes into sums of two-slit terms ($I_3 = 0$); as a consequence,
the intensity patterns from three- and four-slit configurations span \emph{no new
dimensions} beyond those already spanned by two-slit configurations.  This predicts
$\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = n^2 - 1$,
where $\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}$ stacks measurements from all slit-subset
configurations.  A theory admitting higher-order interference would violate this
equality: $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) > \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2)$.
We show that the stacked-matrix approach yields a conclusive rank test with only
three relative phases $\{0,\pi/2,\pi\}$ per slit---a minimal and experimentally
accessible set.  Preliminary results on simulated data confirm
$\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = 15 = n^2-1$
for a four-slit system ($n=4$), consistent with the absence of higher-order
interference.
\end{abstract}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background}

\subsection{Historical approach: the Sorkin measure}

Sorkin~\cite{sorkin1994} introduced a quantitative measure $I_3$ of third-order
interference.  Standard QM satisfies $I_3 = 0$; experimental tests~\cite{sinha2010}
have confirmed this to high precision by directly measuring the deviation of
three-slit intensities from the sum of one- and two-slit terms.  While powerful,
this approach is tied to a specific functional form of higher-order interference
and assumes QM as the background theory within which $I_3$ is defined.

\subsection{A broader question: what theory does nature use?}

The framework of Generalised Probabilistic Theories (GPTs) asks a more fundamental
question: rather than testing a specific deviation from QM, can we determine
\emph{which} probabilistic theory nature actually obeys, using only the operational
data?  In the GPT framework, a physical theory is characterised by its state space
and the dimension of that space.  Higher-order interference is not a small
correction to QM---it is a structural property that certain post-quantum theories
possess and QM does not.  If nature allowed higher-order interference, it would
mean that the state space of a multi-slit system has more degrees of freedom than
QM's density matrix can account for.

\subsection{GPT rank as a probe}

In this work we use the \emph{rank} of the measured intensity matrix as a
theory-agnostic probe of the state space dimension.  For an $n$-slit system
described by a density matrix $\rho \in \mathcal{M}_{n}(\mathbb{C})$,
the Born-rule intensity at pixel $j$ under slit-transmission amplitudes
$\mathbf{a}$ and phases $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ is
\begin{equation}
    I_j(\mathbf{a},\boldsymbol{\phi})
    = \sum_{k,\ell} a_k a_\ell \,\rho_{k\ell}\, e^{i(\phi_k-\phi_\ell)} f_{kj}^* f_{\ell j}.
\end{equation}
Because $I_j$ is linear in $\rho$, the intensity matrix $\mathbf{D}$ (rows =
settings, columns = pixels) satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}) \leq n^2 - 1
    \label{eq:rank_qm}
\end{equation}
for any fixed slit subset, since $\rho$ has $n^2 - 1$ real degrees of freedom.
A post-quantum theory with a larger state space would require
$\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}) > n^2 - 1$.

The key prediction of QM is stronger than just a rank bound on individual matrices.
Because QM requires all multi-slit interference to decompose into two-slit terms,
the column space of $\mathbf{D}_3$ and $\mathbf{D}_4$ is entirely contained within
the column space of $\mathbf{D}_2$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = n^2 - 1.
    \label{eq:rank_equal}
\end{equation}
A post-quantum theory admitting higher-order interference would produce 3- or
4-slit patterns with components \emph{orthogonal} to the 2-slit subspace,
violating Eq.~\eqref{eq:rank_equal}:
$\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) > \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2)$.
Measuring this rank equality is therefore a direct, theory-agnostic test of
whether higher-order interference is a property of nature.

\section{Experimental Setup}

The interferometer is a four-path optical network built from eight beam splitters
(H1--H8), as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:setup}.  A laser enters the network and
is successively split into four vertical arms by beam splitters H1--H4 on the
lower rail.  Each arm contains a shutter (Shutter 1--4) that independently
opens or closes the path, followed by a phase controller ($\Phi_1$--$\Phi_4$)
that sets the relative phase of that arm.  The four arms are recombined by beam
splitters H5--H8 on the upper rail, and the output intensity pattern is recorded
on an electron-multiplying ICCD (emICCD) camera.

By selectively opening subsets of the four shutters and cycling through all
phase combinations $\{0, \pi/2, \pi\}^4 = 81$ patterns, we construct the full
intensity matrix $\mathbf{D}$ covering all 15 non-trivial slit configurations
used in the rank analysis.

\section{The Old Approach and Its Limitation}

\subsection{Per-configuration rank test}

The natural first strategy is to measure the intensity matrix for each number of
open slits separately and verify
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_3) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_4) = n^2 - 1 = 15.
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{D}_m$ denotes the data matrix built from all settings with exactly
$m$ slits open.  Equality across $m$ would confirm that no new dimensions are
introduced as more slits are opened---the fingerprint of higher-order interference.

\subsection{The phase-count bottleneck}

Reliably detecting rank 15 in $\mathbf{D}_m$ requires enough rows to populate the
full 15-dimensional subspace.  For $m$ open slits there are $\binom{4}{m}$ shutter
configurations, each combined with all phase patterns.  With $p$ equally-spaced
relative phases per slit the number of distinct rows is
$\binom{4}{m} \times p^4$.  Table~\ref{tab:row_counts} shows the row counts for
$p = 3$ ($\{0, \pi/2, \pi\}$) and $p = 4$ ($\{0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2\}$).

The critical observation is that with $p = 3$ phases, the four-slit matrix
$\mathbf{D}_4$ has only \textbf{81 rows}---barely five times the target rank of~15.
In practice, many of these rows are nearly collinear (the phase grid is coarse),
leaving the effective rank poorly constrained.  Obtaining a clean result for
$\mathbf{D}_4$ alone therefore requires $p = 4$, which adds a significant
experimental overhead (four phase settings per slit, 256 total patterns for the
all-open configuration alone).

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The Proposed Approach: A Stacked All-Configs Matrix}

\subsection{Construction}

Rather than analysing each $\mathbf{D}_m$ separately, we form a single stacked matrix
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{D}_1 \\ \mathbf{D}_2 \\ \mathbf{D}_3 \\ \mathbf{D}_4 \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
containing \emph{all} non-trivial slit configurations (all 15 non-empty subsets of
four slits) combined with the $p=3$ phase set.  The total row count is
$15 \times 81 = 1215$, providing a highly overdetermined system.

\subsection{Why the rank bound still holds}

For any slit subset $S \subseteq \{1,2,3,4\}$, the intensity $I_j(\mathbf{a}_S,\boldsymbol{\phi})$
is still a bilinear function of the same $n\times n$ density matrix $\rho$.  The
column space of $\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}$ is therefore contained in the same
$(n^2-1)$-dimensional subspace as each $\mathbf{D}_m$.  Consequently
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) \leq n^2 - 1 = 15
\end{equation}
under QM.  More importantly, $\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}$ \emph{spans} this subspace
better than any individual $\mathbf{D}_m$: including all shutter combinations
exercises all off-diagonal elements $\rho_{k\ell}$ systematically.

\subsection{The key claim}

If $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = 15$,
this simultaneously confirms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Three- and four-slit intensity patterns are entirely expressible as
          linear combinations of two-slit patterns---the defining signature of
          $I_3 = 0$ (no higher-order interference).
    \item The full state space of the four-slit system is $(n^2-1)$-dimensional,
          consistent with QM and the Born rule.
    \item The result is obtained with three phases per slit, making it directly
          applicable to the existing experimental dataset without requiring a
          fourth phase setting.
\end{enumerate}
Note that observing $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = 15$ alone would
\emph{not} be sufficient: it would only confirm the state-space dimension.  The
key test is that stacking 3- and 4-slit rows does \emph{not} increase the rank
beyond $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2)$.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The Role of rank($\mathbf{D}_1$)}

The one-slit matrix $\mathbf{D}_1$ serves as a \textbf{calibration and sanity check}.

\paragraph{Expected rank.}  With a single slit there is no interference: the
intensity is simply $I_j \propto |f_{kj}|^2$, independent of the phase settings.
Hence $\mathbf{D}_1$ should be rank~1---a single intensity profile scaled by the
(trivial) amplitude.

\paragraph{What a deviation means.}  Any $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_1) > 1$ would
indicate a systematic experimental artefact: crosstalk between nominally-closed
slits, detector non-linearity, or phase-dependent background.  Verifying
$\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_1) = 1$ therefore validates the experimental apparatus
before drawing conclusions from higher-slit matrices.

\paragraph{In the GPT framework.}  A rank-1 one-slit result also confirms that the
single-slit state is a pure state (rank-1 density matrix), consistent with the
assumption that the slits inject coherent modes.  This anchors the theoretical
model used to interpret the multi-slit ranks.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Rank Estimation: GPT-ALS with Cross-Validation}

We estimate the rank of $\mathbf{D}$ using an Alternating Least Squares (ALS)
factorisation
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{D} \approx \mathbf{u}\,\mathbf{V}^\top, \quad
    \mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_s \times K},\;
    \mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{\mathrm{px}} \times K},
\end{equation}
subject to the normalisation constraint $u_{i,0} = 1$ (first GPT coordinate fixed),
with Poisson-weighted residuals
\begin{equation}
    \chi^2/\mathrm{pt} = \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{train}}}
    \sum_{(i,j)\in\mathrm{train}}
    \frac{(D_{ij} - [\mathbf{u}\mathbf{V}^\top]_{ij})^2}{\sigma_{ij}^2},
    \quad
    \sigma_{ij} = \frac{\sqrt{\max(D_{ij}, p_0)}}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{eff}}}},
\end{equation}
where $p_0 = 0.01$ is a floor to prevent division by zero.

Rank selection uses 10-fold cross-validation over randomly assigned \textbf{(row, pixel) pairs}:
all $N_s \times N_{\mathrm{px}}$ entries are randomly partitioned into 10 folds; each fold
is held out as the test set in turn.  Each row therefore contributes pixels to both
training and test, ensuring that the per-row factors $u_i$ are well-determined during
training and evaluated on genuinely held-out pixels.

The true rank appears as the value of $K$ at which the test $\chi^2/\mathrm{pt}$
reaches a minimum near~1 (i.e.\ the model fits the data within Poisson noise
without overfitting).

\section{Roadmap}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textcolor{green!60!black}{\textbf{[Done] Theory: }}
          $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = 15$
          confirmed on simulated Poisson-noisy data (Figure~\ref{fig:rank_sweep},
          Table~\ref{tab:cv_results}).  For $K < 15$ the model underfits---the rank is
          insufficient to capture all interference structure and test $\chi^2/\mathrm{pt}$
          remains high.  At $K = 15$ the test $\chi^2/\mathrm{pt}$ reaches a minimum,
          indicating the model explains the data without excess flexibility.  For $K > 15$
          the model overfits---additional rank captures noise in the training pixels,
          causing test $\chi^2/\mathrm{pt}$ to rise again.  This behaviour is consistent
          across both the 2-slit and all-configs panels, with the minimum occurring at
          $K = 15 = n^2 - 1$ in both cases.

    \item \textbf{Experimental classical light:}
          Run the same GPT rank sweep on experimental camera data (coherent laser source).
          Goal: confirm $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = 15$
          on real optical data.

    \item \textbf{Single-photon source:}
          Bring the gated single-photon camera online.  This requires characterising
          the detector timing, dark counts, and coincidence window to ensure
          shot-noise-limited operation.

    \item \textbf{Experimental quantum light:}
          Repeat the rank sweep with single photons.  A result of
          $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_{\mathrm{all}}) = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{D}_2) = 15$
          in the single-photon regime would constitute direct evidence that higher-order
          interference is absent in quantum light, and that nature's state space is
          consistent with QM rather than a post-quantum GPT.
\end{enumerate}
